🌬️ 4-7-8 Breathing

This technique activates your parasympathetic nervous system, signaling safety to your body.

  1. Exhale completely through your mouth with a whoosh sound
  2. Close your mouth and inhale through your nose for a count of 4
  3. Hold your breath for a count of 7
  4. Exhale completely through your mouth for a count of 8
  5. Repeat this cycle 3-4 times (one breath per minute)
💡 Tip: The exhale is more important than the inhale. Focus on making your exhale longer and complete. You can adjust the counts (3-5-6 or 2-4-6) if the timing feels too long.

Best for: Panic attacks, racing thoughts, bedtime anxiety, moments when you need quick regulation

👁️ 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

This sensory technique anchors you in the present moment, interrupting anxious thoughts about the future.

Name:

  1. 5 things you see: Look around and name 5 specific things (e.g., "blue chair," "shadow on wall")
  2. 4 things you feel: Notice texture and temperature (e.g., "feet on floor," "fabric on skin")
  3. 3 things you hear: Listen for sounds around you (e.g., "traffic," "birds," "fan humming")
  4. 2 things you smell: Notice scents (can light a candle or go outside if needed)
  5. 1 thing you taste: Put something in your mouth (gum, candy, water, mint)
💡 Tip: Go slowly through each sense. The detailed observation is what makes this effective—not speed. If you can't smell or taste easily, you can add a second thing you hear or feel instead.

Best for: Dissociation, racing thoughts, when you feel "stuck in your head," intrusive thoughts

❄️ TIPP Skill

TIPP is designed to quickly interrupt intense emotional reactions by activating the body's survival reflexes.

TIPP stands for:

T - Temperature (Most Effective):

  • Splash very cold water on your face or hold ice cubes on your cheeks
  • Or: Submerge your face in cold water for 30 seconds (activates "dive response")
  • Triggers an immediate calm-down reflex in your nervous system

I - Intense Exercise:

  • Do 2-3 minutes of vigorous exercise: running, jumping jacks, sprinting up stairs
  • Discharges the adrenaline built up by anxiety
  • Best when you have 5-10 minutes and space to move

P - Paced Breathing:

  • Slow, controlled breathing at about 5-6 breaths per minute
  • Inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 6 counts
  • Tells your nervous system to shift out of threat mode

P - Pair Muscle Relaxation:

  • Tense each muscle group for 5 seconds, then release (progressive muscle relaxation)
  • Start with feet and work up to head
  • Creates awareness of body and discharge of tension
💡 Tip: Temperature (especially ice water on face or cold water immersion) is the fastest and most effective part. Try this first when you're in crisis or high distress.

Best for: Intense panic, emotional flooding, suicidal urges, moments when you need immediate rapid relief

✨ ACCEPTS Skill

ACCEPTS is a distraction skill that redirects your attention to meaningful activities and social connection.

ACCEPTS stands for:

A - Activities:

  • Engage in activities that require mental focus: puzzles, games, crafts, reading
  • Physical activities: exercise, dance, sports, gardening
  • Creative: drawing, writing, music, cooking

C - Contributions (Helping Others):

  • Volunteer, help a friend, do an act of service
  • Clean your space, organize something, help someone solve a problem
  • Shifting focus to others' needs can ease self-focused anxiety

C - Comparisons:

  • Notice others around you experiencing normal life
  • Watch a movie or show (neutral or light-hearted, not triggering)
  • Remember: "Others feel anxiety too. I'm not alone in this."

E - Emotions (Opposite Action):

  • Do something that produces a different emotion
  • Listen to uplifting music, watch something funny, be around people you enjoy

P - Pushing Away (Mental Distraction):

  • Actively push thoughts aside: "I'll think about this later"
  • Use imagery: imagine putting worries in a box, closing it, setting it aside
  • Return focus to present task

T - Thoughts (Thought Replacement):

  • Redirect thoughts to something neutral or pleasant
  • Count backwards from 100 by 7s (requires focus)
  • Mentally list categories (animals, capitals, songs)

S - Sensations (Sensory Focus):

  • Engage your senses: taste something strong (lemon, ginger), hold ice, listen to music
  • Take a cold shower, splash water on your face
  • Redirect attention to physical sensations
💡 Tip: Pick 2-3 ACCEPTS strategies that work for you and keep them ready. Having a "go-to" list makes it easier to use when anxiety is high. This works best for moderate anxiety (not peak panic—use TIPP for that).

Best for: Moderate anxiety, rumination, when you need longer-term coping (15 min +), keeping your mind occupied

🌊 Experiencing Anxiety?
You might be experiencing a wave of physical sensations right now—racing heart, shallow breathing, thoughts moving quickly. This is what anxiety can feel like. In the coming moments, how you respond to this experience can make a difference. There are a couple of different paths you might explore. Whatever feels right for you.
👁️ Observe Without Judgment
You're turning toward what's happening instead of turning away. If it feels okay, try gently noticing: Where do you feel this in your body? What thoughts are coming up? What emotion is there? There's no need to change anything right now—sometimes just acknowledging what's present can feel like a small relief.
💚 Self-Compassionate Response
You're offering yourself some gentleness in this moment. This might feel like a kind thought toward yourself, a hand on your heart, or even just allowing yourself to be okay with not being okay. This internal kindness can create a sense of safety in your nervous system.

✓ What Self-Compassion Looks Like:

  • Internal dialogue example: "I'm having a panic attack. This is scary, but it's not dangerous. My body is trying to protect me. I've survived anxiety before, and I'll get through this."
  • Physical gesture: Put your hand on your heart, take a breath, and literally comfort yourself like you would comfort a child
  • Self-talk script: "This is hard right now. It's okay to struggle. I'm doing the best I can. I deserve kindness, especially from myself."

💭 A Powerful Reframing: You Are Not Your Anxiety

  • Separate the person from the anxiety: Your anxious thoughts and feelings are something you're experiencing right now—they're not who you are. You are larger than this moment.
  • Examples of externalizing:
    • "My anxiety is loud right now, but that's just my nervous system in protection mode"
    • "These are anxious thoughts visiting me, not facts about reality"
    • "Anxiety is trying to help, even though it's getting it wrong right now"
  • Why this helps: When you see anxiety as a separate thing (not your core identity), you can be with it differently. You're not broken—you're someone experiencing anxiety right now.

✓ Regulation Techniques to Try:

  • Rhythmic Breathing: Slow, intentional breathing at 5-6 breaths per minute (inhale for 4, exhale for 6)—this tells your nervous system "we're safe"
  • Somatic Grounding: 5-4-3-2-1 technique: notice 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste (brings you back to present)
  • Internal Validation: "This feeling is real and valid. My nervous system is doing what it's designed to do. I'm not broken."
  • Gentle Movement: Stretching, slow walking, or progressive muscle relaxation to discharge the physical activation
  • Self-Soothing Touch: Warm beverage in your hands, soft blanket, holding a pillow, ice on your face—anything tactile that feels comforting
  • Environment: If safe, change your location (go outside, change rooms, change position). Sometimes a physical shift helps the nervous system shift.
🎯 The Recovery Arc - What to Expect
When you respond to anxiety with gentleness instead of judgment, something can shift in your nervous system. You're offering yourself a safety signal. Anxiety might continue to feel intense for a bit—this is okay and doesn't mean anything is wrong.

The Timeline (This Can Vary For Everyone):

  • Early on (0-5 min): You might notice anxiety as you turn toward it. It could feel intense at first—your body hasn't gotten the "it's okay" message yet.
  • Minutes 5-15 or so: Anxiety might peak. This can be the hardest part. If it helps, keep breathing gently, be kind to yourself, and remember this is temporary.
  • Around 15-30 minutes: For some people, it starts to ease. You might notice a slight softening—a little more calm in your breath, or your thoughts slowing down.
  • 30+ minutes: You might feel some relief. Anxiety may still be there, but it often becomes more manageable.
  • Everyone is different: Your timeline might be faster, slower, or different altogether. That's completely okay.

Why This Matters:

  • You get through it. Each time you move toward anxiety instead of away, you're showing your nervous system that you can be with difficult feelings. That experience can help over time.
  • Your nervous system can learn. With practice, anxiety can become less scary. It might take time and repetition, and that's normal.
  • Avoidance can go the other direction. When we run from anxiety, it often feels more threatening next time. Being with it is harder in the moment, but it can help long-term.
⚠️ Self-Critical Pathway (The Hard Way)
Sometimes when anxiety shows up, we also judge ourselves for having it. This creates an extra layer of difficulty. For example: feeling anxious PLUS feeling bad about being anxious. Your nervous system is working hard on two fronts.

What This Looks Like (Real Examples):

  • "I'm anxious" → "I should be able to handle this by now. Something's wrong with me. Why can't I get it together like everyone else?"
  • "My heart is racing" → "I'm having a heart attack. I'm broken. This is never going to end."
  • "I feel uncomfortable" → "I'm pathetic. Normal people don't panic. I'm going crazy."

What Happens When We Add Self-Judgment:

  • You're managing anxiety PLUS a harsh voice. That's harder on your whole system.
  • Recovery can take longer because there's more to work through
  • You might start believing anxiety means something is fundamentally wrong with you (instead of just being a normal human experience)
  • This pattern is very common. You're not alone in doing this. And it can change.
🔄 You Can Redirect This Right Now
Noticing that you're being hard on yourself is already something. That awareness gives you a choice—you don't have to stay in that pattern. You can try something different.

How to Actually Redirect (Concrete Steps):

  • Step 1 - Name it: "Right now, I'm having anxiety AND I'm being critical about having anxiety. That's two problems, not one."
  • Step 2 - Pause the self-talk: Actually say (out loud if safe): "Stop. I don't deserve criticism. I'm struggling, and that's human."
  • Step 3 - Physical comfort: Put your hand on your heart or hug yourself. This activates your nervous system's calm response.
  • Step 4 - Reframe: "Having anxiety doesn't define me. It's a temporary state my body is creating. I've survived difficult feelings before."
  • Step 5 - Use one technique: Pick ONE from the compassionate pathway (breathing, grounding, movement, comfort object)
💡 Real-life example: "I'm panicking about this meeting" + harsh self-talk → "I'm panicking AND I'm being mean to myself. Let me stop. I'm doing my best. Let me breathe for 4 minutes and see if this passes."
🚫 Avoidance Strategy (The Trap)
You're looking for a way to step away from or numb the anxiety. This brings quick relief, which makes a lot of sense. Over time, though, avoidance can sometimes make anxiety bigger, even if it helps right now.

Real Examples of Avoidance:

  • Mental: Dissociation, "zoning out," not thinking about the problem
  • Substances: Alcohol, drugs, overeating, excessive sleeping to escape
  • Behavioral: Calling in sick to work, canceling plans, staying home
  • Distraction: Endless scrolling, binge-watching, extreme busyness to not feel

How Avoidance Can Grow Over Time:

  • Day 1: Anxious about something → Step back from it → Relief (which feels good)
  • Days 3-5: Anxiety builds again → Avoid again → Relief again
  • Week 2+: It might become easier to keep avoiding because you know it works. But anxiety might also show up in new situations
  • Over time: What started as one feared situation can slowly grow. Your world might get smaller, and anxiety might follow you to new places

What Happens In Your Nervous System:

  • When you step away, your nervous system learns: "I was right to be scared of this."
  • Each time you avoid, that pattern can get stronger. Your system holds onto the fear more tightly.
  • Your nervous system learns it's safe through gradual exposure—staying present with something scary and realizing you'll be okay.
  • Avoidance feels better fast, but the anxiety often comes back. Facing it is harder, but it can help more long-term.
🎭 Types of Avoidance
There are different ways people escape or suppress anxiety. Which approach do you typically use?
💤 Dissociation & Emotional Numbing
You might be checking out mentally, stepping away from your body, or using something to help numb the feelings. This brings relief right away, which makes sense. But over time, it can sometimes make anxiety harder to work with.

What Dissociation Looks Like (Real Examples):

  • Feeling "outside your body" or watching yourself from afar
  • Emotional numbness—feeling like you don't care about anything
  • "Zoning out" for hours without awareness of time passing
  • Memory gaps—not remembering what happened during anxiety episodes
  • Robotic autopilot movements (driving, talking) without conscious awareness
  • Using alcohol, drugs, or sleep to escape into unconsciousness

Why This Becomes a Problem:

  • Creates a disconnect between you and your actual experience
  • Over time, dissociation episodes tend to intensify or occur more frequently
  • Prevents your nervous system from learning that anxiety is survivable—you never actually experience yourself surviving it
  • Can lead to substance dependence if using drugs/alcohol as dissociation tools
  • May result in self-harm you don't remember or injuries sustained without noticing

⚠️ When to Seek Professional Help: If dissociation is happening frequently, involuntarily, or lasting for hours, this is not something to manage alone. Please reach out to a therapist or psychiatrist who specializes in trauma/dissociation. This is treatable, but it needs professional support. Types of effective therapy: EMDR, trauma-focused CBT, DBT.

🎮 Redirecting Attention
Shifting your focus to something that genuinely holds your attention. This is different from forcing the anxiety away—you're choosing to engage with something else. This works better when anxiety is lower; when it's intense, other tools might help more.

✓ Good Distractions (Engage Your Brain):

  • Puzzle games, crosswords, sudoku (requires focus)
  • Creative activities: drawing, painting, crafts, music, writing
  • Physical activities: exercise, yoga, dancing, playing sports
  • Social engagement: phone call with a friend, online game with others, group activity
  • Learning: podcast about something interesting, educational video, book

❌ Poor Distractions (Passive/Avoidant):

  • Mindless scrolling (doesn't actually engage your brain)
  • Passive TV watching (too easy to slip back into anxiety thoughts)
  • Sleeping excessively (avoidance, not distraction)
  • Substance use to numb (this is dissociation, not distraction)
💡 Something to know: Distraction works better when anxiety is lighter. When anxiety is really intense, you might need something more direct like breathing or grounding. It's okay to use different tools for different moments.
🚪 Situation/Context Avoidance
You're stepping away from situations that bring anxiety. This brings relief, which is understandable. Over time, it can sometimes make anxiety bigger, even though it helps right now.

How the Avoidance Cycle Develops:

  • First avoidance: You escape the situation → relief → your brain marks this as successful
  • Reinforcement: Next time, avoidance feels like the obvious choice
  • Expansion: Related situations also start feeling unsafe, so you avoid those too
  • Result: Your accessible world gradually shrinks, anxiety grows stronger
🌟 You Now Have a Map
You've explored how anxiety shows up and what different responses look like. There are patterns here that you can work with. Practicing new responses, even small ones, can help your nervous system learn over time.

Some Things to Keep in Mind:

  • Anxiety Served a Purpose: Your nervous system developed anxiety to protect you. It's trying to help, even when it feels overwhelming. It's not broken—it's just working overtime sometimes.
  • You Have Choices in the Moment: When anxiety shows up, there are different ways to respond. Some responses might feel gentler than others. Over time, you can notice which ones feel right for you.
  • Kindness Can Help: When you offer yourself gentleness during anxiety, it can feel different than criticism. Many people find this helps, even if it feels small at first.
  • Avoidance Has Tradeoffs: Stepping away from anxiety feels better right away. But sometimes anxiety can grow if we keep avoiding. It can be worth exploring other options, at your own pace.
  • Change Takes Time: New responses don't usually feel automatic right away. They often become easier with practice. There's no rush—you can go at your own pace.
  • Anxiety Has a Natural Course: Most anxiety doesn't last forever—it tends to ease on its own. Everyone's timeline is different, and that's okay.
  • Support Can Help: If anxiety is making daily life really hard, talking to a therapist can be useful. They can help you understand your own patterns and teach you skills that fit your life. It's totally okay to reach out.
  • This Tool is a Starting Point: The goal isn't to never feel anxious—it's to be able to live your life even when anxiety is there. This flowchart is one resource among many.

💙 If It Helps: When you're ready, you might try one thing from this tool—even something small. There's no pressure. What matters is what feels right for you.
